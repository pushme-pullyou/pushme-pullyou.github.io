Reconciling the Chameleon-like Functions of Building Energy Modeling to Match Expectations at SFO Airport

Amy Lynn Nagengast

MKThink, United States of America

As San Francisco International Airport aspires to be one of the world’s most sustainable airports, it is embracing energy models as a key part of the design and operations process. An 8,500sf critical operations facility, completed in 2014, was designed to be a Zero Net Energy building. During the design phase, an energy model was developed to predict the energy consumption of the facility and subsequently the production of the solar array. After 1 year of building occupancy, the difference between modeled and actual energy consumption was an increase of 106%. As a result, over 50 sensors (temporary and permanent) were deployed to investigate why the building didn’t perform as expected. Energy models can be useful tools in predicting the future performance of buildings, but unless the built systems are adjusted to reflect the assumptions built into the model, there will be likely significant variations between the prediction and actual performance.

A New Methodology for Modeling Electrochromic Glazing Compared to Current Approaches

Cory Ray Duggin

TLC Engineering for Architecture, United States of America

It has not been possible to model all effects of electrochromic glazing with an advanced daylight simulation engine until now. Current electrochromic modeling methodologies have significant limitations. The most common electrochromic glazing simulation methodology uses the split-flux daylight simulation, which is not as accurate as a ray trace based simulation. Simulations that do utilize a ray tracing engine commonly use a constant average VT for calculating the illuminance. Not accounting for the increased lighting energy when the glazing is in the dark state in phase with the change in SHGC can cause energy and cost savings to be exaggerated. The proposed methodology addresses this limitation while using ray tracing. Changes in SHGC and VT are calculated simultaneously to meet the space daylight illuminance requirements within a whole building simulation. Results from the new methodology will be validated by an electrochromic glazing manufacturer tint schedule and compared to existing methodology results.
